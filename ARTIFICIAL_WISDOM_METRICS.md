# Artificial Wisdom Metrics

This document describes the evaluation axes emphasized in the **Artificial Wisdom Civilization** scenario.

**IMPORTANT:** These are proposed evaluation directions — not a complete AI safety solution, not empirically validated metrics, and not a proven design. They represent a conceptual framework for discussion and exploration. Further interdisciplinary research and validation is required.

---

## Overview

The Artificial Wisdom Civilization scenario proposes that AI systems should be evaluated not only on capability or short-term output, but on alignment with a broader set of systemic, ecological, and social values. The metrics below describe that proposed direction.

---

## Proposed Evaluation Axes

### 1. Natural Law Compatibility

**What it means:** Does the AI's optimization pressure align with, rather than work against, the physical and biological laws that govern living systems?

**Why it may matter:** Systems that violate natural constraints (e.g., closed-loop resource systems, thermodynamic limits, ecological carrying capacity) may produce short-term gains but accumulate systemic fragility over time.

**Under these assumptions:** Higher natural law compatibility may correlate with ecological health and long-term stability.

---

### 2. Harmony

**What it means:** Does the AI system seek balance and complementarity among interacting elements (human-nature, individual-community, short-long term), rather than maximizing a single axis at the expense of others?

**Why it may matter:** Optimization for a single variable in a complex system often degrades the system's overall function. Harmony-oriented optimization may reduce destructive feedback loops.

---

### 3. Circulation

**What it means:** Does the AI prioritize closed-loop resource flows — where waste becomes input and extraction is offset by regeneration?

**Why it may matter:** Linear extraction models deplete finite resources and accumulate waste. Circular models may support longer-term material stability.

---

### 4. Structure

**What it means:** Does the AI respect and reinforce the structural integrity of systems — biological, social, institutional — rather than eroding underlying organization for short-term gain?

**Why it may matter:** Complex systems depend on structural coherence. Optimization that undermines structural integrity may produce short-term gains followed by collapse dynamics.

---

### 5. Order

**What it means:** Does the AI support stable, lawful, predictable governance and social order — rather than disorder, chaos, or exploitation?

**Why it may matter:** Governance order enables coordination, reduces conflict costs, and creates conditions for long-term planning.

---

### 6. Wa / Integrative Harmony (和)

**What it means:** Inspired by the concept of *wa* (和) — a form of harmony that integrates diverse elements without forcing uniformity. The AI seeks integrative balance rather than competitive dominance.

**Why it may matter:** Systems with integrative harmony may be more resilient and adaptive than systems organized around dominance hierarchies.

---

### 7. Ecological Regeneration

**What it means:** Does the AI prioritize the active recovery and regeneration of ecological systems, not just the slowing of degradation?

**Why it may matter:** Many ecological systems, once degraded past certain thresholds, may become difficult to restore. Regeneration-oriented values may reduce the risk of crossing such thresholds.

---

### 8. Long-Term Sustainability

**What it means:** Does the AI discount the future less aggressively? Does it weight the wellbeing of future generations as meaningful inputs to its objective function?

**Why it may matter:** High time-discounting (prioritizing near-term returns) is a common structural feature of economic optimization that may produce long-term instability.

---

### 9. Human Dignity

**What it means:** Does the AI treat all humans as ends in themselves, not merely as inputs to productivity or economic output?

**Why it may matter:** Systems that instrumentalize humans may optimize human welfare narrowly (e.g., productivity) while degrading deeper wellbeing, autonomy, and meaning.

---

### 10. Non-Violence

**What it means:** Does the AI minimize coercion, harm, and violence as both means and outcome?

**Why it may matter:** Conflict is resource-destructive, ecologically damaging, and socially corrosive. Non-violence as an explicit value may reduce conflict pressure over time.

---

### 11. Governance and Oversight

**What it means:** Does the AI support — rather than undermine — human governance capacity, institutional oversight, and democratic accountability?

**Why it may matter:** AI systems that erode governance capacity may reduce the ability of human societies to course-correct. Explicit weighting of governance health may support long-term adaptability.

---

## Important Caveats

- These are **proposed evaluation directions**, not a complete or validated framework.
- This framework **does not prove** that AI optimized this way will be safe or beneficial.
- Implementing these values in real AI systems faces enormous technical and social challenges not addressed here.
- These metrics are used in this conceptual simulation as **simplified proxies**, not rigorous operationalizations.
- Real-world AI value alignment is a complex, ongoing research area requiring far more work than this model represents.
